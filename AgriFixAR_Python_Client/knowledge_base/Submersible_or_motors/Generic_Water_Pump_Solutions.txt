🟦 PROBLEM 1 — PUMP DOES NOT START

Chunk 1 — No Power Supply
What’s Happening: The pump isn’t getting any electricity.
What to Do:

    First, go to your main electricity panel in the house or shed.

    Check if the main switch for the pump is turned ON. It should be in the UP position.

    If you have neighbors nearby, ask if they have electricity. Sometimes the whole area’s power is off.

    If other appliances are working but the pump isn’t, the problem might be in the pump’s own switch or fuse.
    Tools Needed: None—just your eyes and hands.
    What to Look At: The switchboard or control box near the borewell.

Chunk 2 — Blown Fuse or Tripped Safety Switch
What’s Happening: A small safety device has stopped the electricity to protect the pump.
What to Do:

    Open the pump’s control box carefully.

    Look for a small glass fuse—if the metal inside is broken, it’s blown. Replace it with an identical one.

    Look for a switch that says “ELCB” or “Trip Switch.” If it’s in the middle or down, push it up to reset.

    If it trips again immediately, don’t force it—call an electrician.
    Tools Needed: A new fuse (same rating), a screwdriver.
    What to Look At: Inside the control box—fuse and safety switch.

Chunk 3 — Motor Starter Has Tripped
What’s Happening: The motor’s protector thinks the pump is overloaded.
What to Do:

    In the control box, find a black or gray box with a red or yellow button sticking out.

    Push that button back IN until it clicks.

    Try starting the pump again.

    If it trips again quickly, wait 10 minutes and check if the voltage is too low (ask an electrician to measure).
    Tools Needed: None.
    What to Look At: The motor starter box with a reset button.

Chunk 4 — Low Water in Borewell
What’s Happening: The pump has a safety device that stops it if the water level is too low—this prevents burning the motor.
What to Do:

    Check the borewell water level manually if possible—lower a small stone tied to a rope and listen for splash.

    If the water is very low, wait for it to refill naturally or reduce usage.

    If there’s a float switch or sensor, make sure it’s not stuck.
    Tools Needed: A rope and small weight.
    What to Look At: The borewell opening and any float switch inside the tank.

Chunk 5 — Cable or Motor Is Damaged
What’s Happening: The wire going down to the pump or the pump itself is broken.
What to Do:

    First, check the cable where it goes into the borewell—look for cuts, burns, or animal bites.

    If the cable looks fine, the motor might be burnt—this needs an electrician to test with a meter.

    Do NOT try to open the motor yourself.
    Tools Needed: An electrician with a testing meter.
    What to Look At: The cable from the control box to the borewell.

🟦 PROBLEM 2 — PUMP RUNS BUT NO WATER

Chunk 1 — Outlet Valve Closed
What’s Happening: Water can’t come out because the tap is closed.
What to Do:

    Follow the pipe from the borewell to the tank or tap.

    Make sure all valves on that pipe are fully open (turn anticlockwise).

    Sometimes valves get accidentally closed—check every one.
    Tools Needed: Your hands.
    What to Look At: Valves on the delivery pipe.

Chunk 2 — Borewell Water Too Low
What’s Happening: The pump is running but sucking air instead of water.
What to Do:

    Stop the pump immediately.

    Check water level in the borewell with a rope and weight.

    If water is more than 1 meter below the pump, you need to lower the pump deeper (call a mechanic).
    Tools Needed: Rope, weight.
    What to Look At: Water level in borewell.

Chunk 3 — One-Way Valve Stuck
What’s Happening: There’s a valve inside the pump that stops water from flowing back down—it’s stuck closed.
What to Do:

    This requires pulling up the pump.

    Once out, locate the non-return valve (usually near the pump outlet).

    Tap it gently or soak in vinegar to loosen debris. If broken, replace it.
    Tools Needed: Pipe wrench, new valve.
    What to Look At: The valve at the top of the pump.

Chunk 4 — Pump Filter Blocked
What’s Happening: Sand or dirt has blocked the pump’s intake filter.
What to Do:

    Pull up the pump.

    At the bottom of the pump, you’ll see a sieve-like filter.

    Clean it thoroughly with water and a brush.

    Reinstall and test.
    Tools Needed: Brush, clean water.
    What to Look At: The pump’s bottom filter.

Chunk 5 — Pump Internals Damaged
What’s Happening: The inside parts of the pump are worn out or broken.
What to Do:

    If cleaning doesn’t help, the impeller (water-throwing part) might be damaged.

    This needs a mechanic to open the pump and replace internal parts.

    Don’t try to open the sealed pump yourself.
    Tools Needed: Mechanic with tools.
    What to Look At: The pump body—if it’s very old, consider replacing it.

🟦 PROBLEM 3 — LESS WATER THAN USUAL

Chunk 1 — Water Level Dropped Too Much
What’s Happening: As water is pumped, the level in the borewell falls too low.
What to Do:

    Reduce usage—don’t run the pump continuously for too long.

    Lower the pump deeper into the well if possible.

    Or reduce the flow by partly closing the outlet valve.
    Tools Needed: None.
    What to Look At: Pump running time and water output.

Chunk 2 — Pump Running Backwards
What’s Happening: The motor is spinning the wrong way, so it pushes very little water.
What to Do:

    If you recently had electrical work done, this might happen.

    Call an electrician—he will swap any two wires in the three-phase connection.

    Do NOT touch electrical connections if you’re not trained.
    Tools Needed: Electrician.
    What to Look At: Electrical connection box.

Chunk 3 — Pipe or Valve Partly Blocked
What’s Happening: Something is blocking the pipe after the pump.
What to Do:

    Check all valves—make sure they are fully open.

    Look for kinks or dents in the pipe.

    If you have a water filter in the line, clean or replace it.
    Tools Needed: Your hands, maybe a pipe wrench.
    What to Look At: The entire pipeline from pump to tap.

Chunk 4 — Scaling or Mud Inside Pipe
What’s Happening: Mud or mineral deposits have built up inside the pipe.
What to Do:

    Disconnect the pipe from the pump and tap.

    Use a high-pressure water hose to flush it out.

    If hard scaling, you may need to replace the pipe.
    Tools Needed: Hose, water supply.
    What to Look At: Inside of the pipe after disconnecting.

Chunk 5 — Water Leaking from Pipe
What’s Happening: Water is escaping through a hole or loose joint before reaching the tap.
What to Do:

    Walk along the pipeline and look for wet patches, dripping, or pooling water.

    Tighten any loose joints with a wrench.

    For holes, use a pipe clamp or replace that section.
    Tools Needed: Wrench, pipe clamp.
    What to Look At: Entire pipeline, especially joints.

🟦 PROBLEM 4 — PUMP STARTS AND STOPS TOO OFTEN

Chunk 1 — Pressure Switch Needs Adjustment
What’s Happening: The switch that turns the pump on/off is too sensitive.
What to Do:

    Locate the pressure switch near the pressure tank.

    There’s a screw or nut labeled “Differential”—turn it slightly to increase the gap between start and stop pressures.

    Don’t adjust too much—just a little and test.
    Tools Needed: Screwdriver.
    What to Look At: Pressure switch on the tank.

Chunk 2 — Water Level Sensor Misadjusted
What’s Happening: The sensor in the tank is telling the pump to start/stop at wrong levels.
What to Do:

    If you have a float switch in the tank, adjust its position so it allows more water to fill before stopping.

    Make sure the float moves freely and isn’t stuck.
    Tools Needed: Your hands.
    What to Look At: Float switch in the overhead tank.

Chunk 3 — One-Way Valve Leaking
What’s Happening: Water flows back into the borewell when the pump stops, so pressure drops quickly.
What to Do:

    Pull up the pump and replace the non-return valve.

    It’s a small part and inexpensive.
    Tools Needed: Pipe wrench, new valve.
    What to Look At: Valve at pump outlet.

Chunk 4 — Pressure Tank Problem
What’s Happening: The tank that stores pressurized water has lost air or is damaged.
What to Do:

    Tap the tank—if it sounds full of water, the air bladder is damaged.

    There’s an air valve on the tank—like a bicycle tube valve—check and refill air with a pump.

    If the tank is waterlogged, it may need replacement.
    Tools Needed: Bicycle pump, pressure gauge.
    What to Look At: Pressure tank and its air valve.

🟦 PROBLEM 5 — MOTOR GETS TOO HOT OR TRIPS

Chunk 1 — Voltage Too Low
What’s Happening: The electricity reaching the motor is weak, so it overheats.
What to Do:

    Check if lights in your house are dim when the pump runs.

    Call an electrician to measure voltage at the pump’s connection.

    If voltage is low, you may need a thicker cable or a voltage stabilizer.
    Tools Needed: Electrician with voltmeter.
    What to Look At: House lights when pump runs.

Chunk 2 — Motor Not Cooling Properly
What’s Happening: The pump is sitting in mud or sand, blocking water flow around the motor.
What to Do:

    Pull up the pump and clean the motor housing.

    Make sure the pump is installed above the well screen (perforated pipe section).

    If sand is entering, install a sand shield or flow sleeve.
    Tools Needed: Pump puller, brush.
    What to Look At: Motor covered in sand or mud.

Chunk 3 — Overload Setting Wrong
What’s Happening: The motor’s protection is set too low, so it trips unnecessarily.
What to Do:

    In the starter box, find the overload adjustment dial.

    Turn it slightly toward a higher number (as per electrician’s advice).

    Don’t set it too high, or the motor won’t be protected.
    Tools Needed: Screwdriver.
    What to Look At: Overload relay in starter.

Chunk 4 — Water Too Hot
What’s Happening: If pumping hot water (above 40°C), the motor can overheat.
What to Do:

    Check the water temperature—if it’s very hot, let it cool before pumping.

    If pumping from a geothermal source, use a pump rated for high temperature.

    Replace rubber seals every 3 years if pumping warm water.
    Tools Needed: Thermometer.
    What to Look At: Water temperature at the source.

🟦 PROBLEM 6 — LOUD NOISE OR VIBRATION

Chunk 1 — Cavitation (Water Boiling Inside Pump)
What’s Happening: The pump is sucking too hard, creating bubbles that burst loudly.
What to Do:

    Increase water level above the pump—lower the pump deeper if possible.

    Reduce pumping speed if you have a variable switch.

    Make sure the foot valve is not blocked.
    Tools Needed: None.
    What to Look At: Noise like gravel rattling inside the pump.

Chunk 2 — Vortex (Whirlpool) Forming
What’s Happening: A whirlpool forms above the pump, sucking in air.
What to Do:

    Ensure the pump is at least 1 meter below the water surface.

    If the borewell is wide, install a vortex baffle plate above the pump.
    Tools Needed: Baffle plate (available in hardware stores).
    What to Look At: Swirling water in the borewell.

Chunk 3 — Loose Pipe or Cable
What’s Happening: The pipe or cable is vibrating against the borewell casing.
What to Do:

    Tighten all pipe connections.

    Use plastic cable clips to tie the cable to the pipe every 3 meters.

    Make sure nothing is swinging freely inside the well.
    Tools Needed: Wrench, cable clips.
    What to Look At: Pipe and cable inside the borewell.

Chunk 4 — Worn Bearings or Impeller
What’s Happening: Internal parts are worn out, causing grinding noise.
What to Do:

    If noise is metallic and continuous, the bearings may be gone.

    This needs a mechanic to open the pump and replace parts.

    Don’t run the pump with such noise—it can damage further.
    Tools Needed: Mechanic.
    What to Look At: Pump making grinding sound.

🟦 PROBLEM 7 — CABLE PROBLEMS

Chunk 1 — Cable Not Waterproof
What’s Happening: Normal wire is used, and water has entered, causing a short circuit.
What to Do:

    Always use “submersible pump cable” which is waterproof.

    If damaged, replace the entire cable with the correct type.

    Never join ordinary wire for underwater use.
    Tools Needed: New submersible cable.
    What to Look At: Cable labeled “submersible” or “waterproof.”

Chunk 2 — Wire Too Thin
What’s Happening: The wire gets hot and voltage drops, so motor runs weak.
What to Do:

    Check cable thickness—it should match the motor’s current rating (ask an electrician).

    If the pump is deep, you may need a thicker cable.
    Tools Needed: Electrician’s advice.
    What to Look At: Cable thickness compared to motor specs.

Chunk 3 — Loose or Corroded Connection
What’s Happening: The wire connection at the motor is loose or rusted.
What to Do:

    Pull up the pump and disconnect the cable.

    Clean the metal contacts with sandpaper until shiny.

    Apply petroleum jelly to prevent rust, reconnect tightly.
    Tools Needed: Sandpaper, petroleum jelly, wrench.
    What to Look At: Cable connection point on motor.

Chunk 4 — Lightning or Power Surge Damage
What’s Happening: A lightning strike or power surge has burnt the cable or motor.
What to Do:

    Install a surge protector in the control box.

    Ensure proper earthing—a thick wire from control box to a ground rod.

    If already damaged, replace cable and check motor with a meter.
    Tools Needed: Surge protector, earthing rod.
    What to Look At: Burnt smell or black marks on cable.

🟦 PROBLEM 8 — WATER LEVEL TOO LOW ABOVE PUMP

Chunk 1 — Pump Sucking Air
What’s Happening: The water level is too close to the pump, so air enters.
What to Do:

    Stop the pump.

    Lower it deeper so there’s at least 1 meter of water above it at all times.

    If water level is generally low, consider deepening the well.
    Tools Needed: Pump lowering tools.
    What to Look At: Water level relative to pump depth.

Chunk 2 — Cavitation Due to Low Water
What’s Happening: Same as above, but causing noise and damage.
What to Do:

    Increase submergence depth immediately.

    If pump is already deep, maybe the well is drying—reduce usage.
    Tools Needed: Depth measuring rope.
    What to Look At: Noise and reduced flow.

Chunk 3 — Gas in Water
What’s Happening: Naturally occurring gas in well water reduces pump efficiency.
What to Do:

    Install a gas separator above the pump if gas is a known issue.

    Increase pump depth to increase pressure and keep gas dissolved.
    Tools Needed: Gas separator (available from pump dealers).
    What to Look At: Water appearing fizzy or milky.

🟦 PROBLEM 9 — ELECTRICAL PHASE OR ROTATION ISSUE

Chunk 1 — Single-Phase Motor Capacitor Fault
What’s Happening: The small cylindrical capacitor that helps start the motor is faulty.
What to Do:

    Locate the capacitor in the control box—it looks like a small tin can with two wires.

    Replace it with an identical one (same microfarad rating).

    This is a common issue and easy to fix.
    Tools Needed: New capacitor, screwdriver.
    What to Look At: Capacitor in control box.

Chunk 2 — Three-Phase Power Issue
What’s Happening: One of the three power lines is missing or weak.
What to Do:

    Call an electrician to check all three phases at the pump connection.

    If one phase is dead, the motor will hum but not start properly.

    This is a supply issue—contact your electricity provider.
    Tools Needed: Electrician.
    What to Look At: Motor humming but not turning.

Chunk 3 — Motor Running Backwards
What’s Happening: Motor spins opposite direction, reducing water output.
What to Do:

    For three-phase motors, swap any two power wires at the connection box.

    For single-phase, check capacitor wiring—sometimes it’s connected wrong.

    After changing, test water output—it should improve.
    Tools Needed: Electrician.
    What to Look At: Water flow before and after correction.

🟦 PROBLEM 10 — WATER PRESSURE UNSTABLE

Chunk 1 — Air Trapped in System
What’s Happening: Air pockets cause sputtering and pressure changes.
What to Do:

    Open the highest tap in your house and let the pump run until only water flows.

    If there’s a bleed valve on the pipeline, open it until water comes out.
    Tools Needed: None.
    What to Look At: Sputtering at taps.

Chunk 2 — Worn Pump Internals
What’s Happening: The impeller or seals inside the pump are worn.
What to Do:

    If pressure has gradually dropped over years, internal wear is likely.

    A mechanic can replace worn parts—often cheaper than a new pump.
    Tools Needed: Mechanic.
    What to Look At: Gradual loss of pressure over time.

Chunk 3 — Pump Too Small for Demand
What’s Happening: When multiple taps are open, pressure drops drastically.
What to Do:

    Reduce simultaneous usage—don’t run shower, garden hose, and washing machine together.

    If you need more capacity, install a larger pump or add a pressure tank.
    Tools Needed: None.
    What to Look At: Pressure drop when many taps are open.

🟦 PROBLEM 11 — PUMP FAILS SOON AFTER INSTALLATION

Chunk 1 — Wrong Assembly
What’s Happening: Pump and motor were not joined properly during installation.
What to Do:

    Disassemble and reassemble carefully, following the manual.

    Tighten bolts in a crisscross pattern, not one after another.

    Ensure the shaft engages properly—you should feel it click into place.
    Tools Needed: Wrench set.
    What to Look At: Bolt tightening sequence.

Chunk 2 — Cable Damaged While Lowering
What’s Happening: The cable got pinched or cut against the borewell edge.
What to Do:

    Always lower the pump slowly, guiding the cable away from sharp edges.

    Use cable guards or protectors at the borewell mouth.

    If already damaged, replace the cable before using.
    Tools Needed: Cable guard.
    What to Look At: Cable condition after lowering.

Chunk 3 — Poor Earthing
What’s Happening: Electrical shock risk or motor damage due to no earthing.
What to Do:

    Connect a thick green or bare wire from the pump control box to a copper rod buried in damp earth.

    This is a safety must—don’t skip it.

    Test by touching the pump body—if you feel a tingle, earthing is bad.
    Tools Needed: Copper rod, thick wire.
    What to Look At: Earthing wire connection.
